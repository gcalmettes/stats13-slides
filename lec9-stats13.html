Goal of statistics

To answer a question about a population (of data).

In a perfect world, we would have access to the full population so we would be able to answer the question based on data collected on all the observational units of the population. We would know the value of the true parameter we are intested in.

But usually we just have one sample.

Statistic is about using that single sample to make inference about the population parameter.

Population data

Let's consider that we are in charge of running a new health campaign that targets the students of a particular school. This school has 500 students. We want to know if using TV ads would be an efficient way of making the students aware of this new campaign.

One parameter that could be of interest to answer this question is the average time a student spend per quarter watching TV.

Sampling distribution

We only have a limited budget ($\$$60) to investigate our question ("What is the value of $\mu$?"), and it costs $\$$10/student to collect information.

We randomly select 6 students in the school to obtain a representative sample we can work with. From this sample (n=6) we calculate the average time spent per student watching TV per quarter (if representative sample, $\bar{x}\approx\mu$).

If we had randomly selected another sample, the value of $\bar{x}$ obtained would have been a bit different (but still $\bar{x}\approx\mu$).

Lots of possible different samples from the population, lots of slightly different values for the statistics of interest ($\bar{x}\approx\mu$).
All of those possible statistics of interest define the sampling distribution of the parameter.

Statistical test/procedure

Any statistical test/procedure relies on the sampling distribution:

In the case of a null hypothesis significance testing, we are interested in the sampling distribution of the null hypothesis.

When we want to determine an estimate (confidence intervals) of a parameter of interest, we are interested in the sampling distribution of the statistic.

What is the probability that the statistic I calculated from my sample(s) comes from this sampling distribution of the null hypothesis? (p-value)

What would be a range of possible values for the true value of the parameter of interest? (This estimate depends on how variable the sampling distribution of the statistic is)

NHST: One sample

When we perform a null hypothesis test on a one-sample dataset, we are usually interested in knowing if the parameter of the population our sample is coming from is different from a specific value.

For a categorical variable, the sampling distribution we are comparing to is usually the one of a statistic governed by a chance model with a specific probability of success.

For a quantitative variable, unless we do have a specific model for the null hypothesis we want to compare our sample with, then we cannot really generate a reliable null sampling distribution without making strong assumptions.

For one proportion, the population we are interested in is usually the "chance process" population (we want to compare our result to a random process).

If the phenomena we are studying is governed by chance, what the sampling distribution of our parameter of interest would look like?

Because a proportion is a probability, then it is easy to model that using resampling. What would be the possible proportions we would observe for x number of events?

One quantitative variable

Not really a chance process. Unless we do have a model for a null hypothesis, then we cannot really generate a model without making assumptions.

But if we do have a model, then we can do it. (ex: we have a machine generating series of numbers, and we want to know if a particular series of numbers that we found could come from this machine.)

Two groups

In the case of two samples, we usually want if they are differents or not.

So statistic of interest is the difference (or ratio).

But then the same principle apply, we need the sampling distribution and see where in this sampling distribution our original observation falls.

Because we have two group, if the question is whether the two groups are the same (=if they could be samples coming from the same population), then is it easy to model this null hypothesis by gathering the two groups into a single population

So what's the big deal with Confidence intervals?

NHST is all nice and stuff, but in the end, we spend time answering the wrong question: instead on focusing on what our data are, we focus on where our data would stand if the null hypothesis was true.

Confidence intervals is a way at looking at the same thing, but using what you know, not "what would be if"
