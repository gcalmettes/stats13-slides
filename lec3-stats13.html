Can dolphins communicate?

Dr. Jarvins Bastian's experiment (1964)

In 1964, Dr. Jarvis Bastian trained two dolphins, Buzz and Doris, to push one of two switches in a pool. If he shined a constant light on them, the dolphins were trained to push the switch on the right to get a fish. If the light blinked, the dolphins were trained to push the switch on the left. The dolphins had no problems learning this behavior.

In one phase of the study, Dr. Bastian had Buzz attempt to push the correct button a total of 16 different times.

2 possible explanations

Buzz is just guessing (his probability of a correct button push is 0.50) and he got really lucky in these 16 attempts

Buzz is doing something other than just guessing (his probability of a correct button push is more than 0.50)

The key question here is to determine what results would occur in the long-run under the assumption that Buzz is just guessing.

=>

We call this assumption of random guess by Buzz the null hypothesis (or null model)

How could we decide between our 2 hypotheses?

Long-run pattern for 16 coin-flips

We really need to simulate this random selection process hundreds, preferably thousands of times. This would be very tedious and time-consuming with coins, so we'll turn to technology.

How many of these 1000 simulations produced 15 or more correct choices by Buzz?
2 out of 1000

What is the corresponding proportion of simulations that produced such an extreme result?
$p=\frac{2}{1000}=0.002$

1000 is a number large enough to give us a fairly accurate sense of the long-run behavior for the number of heads in 16 tosses.

p-value

A p-value is the probability of obtaining a result as extreme as the one observed, assuming that the null hypothesis is true.

A small p-value casts doubt on the null hypothesis/model used to perform the calculation (in this case, that Buzz was chosing a button randomly).

A p-value

$\leq{0.10}$
$\leq{0.05}$
$\leq{0.01}$
